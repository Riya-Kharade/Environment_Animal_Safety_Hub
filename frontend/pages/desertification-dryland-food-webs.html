Primary Soil Degradation Processes

Water Erosion

Heavy rainfall washes away topsoil, particularly on degraded landscapes without vegetation cover.

Wind Erosion

Dry, bare soils are easily picked up by wind, creating dust storms and further soil loss.

Nutrient Depletion

Continuous cropping without adequate fertilization depletes essential nutrients from the soil.

Soil Compaction

Heavy machinery and overgrazing compress soil particles, reducing water infiltration and root growth.

Organic Matter Loss

Reduced plant growth means less organic material returns to the soil, decreasing fertility.

Salinity Increase

Poor irrigation practices bring salts to the surface, making soil inhospitable for most plants.

Regional Case Studies

Sahara Desert Expansion

The Sahara Desert has expanded southward by hundreds of kilometers in recent decades, consuming productive grasslands and savannas.

Impact: Loss of pastoral livelihoods, increased dust storms, and displacement of millions of people.

Gobi Desert Encroachment

In Asia, the Gobi Desert is expanding northward, threatening agricultural regions and wildlife habitats in Mongolia and China.

Impact: Reduced biodiversity, economic losses in agriculture, and increased frequency of sandstorms.

Australian Arid Zone

Overgrazing and climate change have degraded vast areas of Australia's interior, leading to soil erosion and vegetation loss.

Impact: Extinction of native species, reduced water availability, and increased fire risk.

American Southwest

Historical overgrazing and modern climate change are accelerating desertification in the southwestern United States.

Impact: Loss of ranching productivity, increased water scarcity, and urban dust issues.
