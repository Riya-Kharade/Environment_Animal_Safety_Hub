What is Immunity?

Biological Defense
"The immune system is a complex network of cells, tissues, and organs that work together to defend the body against attacks by 'foreign' invaders, primarily microbes—tiny organisms such as bacteria, parasites, and fungi." - National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases

Physical Barriers

Skin, mucous membranes, chemical barriers

95% effective First line

Cellular Defenses

White blood cells, phagocytes, natural killer cells

Rapid response Second line

Humoral Defenses

Antibodies, complement proteins, cytokines

Specific Third line

Immune Cell Types

Meet the specialized defenders of the animal body

Macrophage

Phagocytic Cell

Large phagocytes that engulf pathogens and debris, present antigens to lymphocytes

Size 15-80 μm
Lifespan Months to years
Location All tissues
Phagocytosis Antigen presentation Cytokine production

B Lymphocyte

Antibody Producer

Produce antibodies and differentiate into memory B cells and plasma cells

Size 7-8 μm
Lifespan Weeks to years
Location Lymph nodes, spleen
Antibody production Memory formation Antigen recognition

T Lymphocyte

Cell Mediated Immunity

Coordinate immune response, kill infected cells, and regulate immune activity

Size 7-8 μm
Lifespan Years
Location Blood, lymph
Cytotoxic killing Helper functions Memory formation

Neutrophil

First Responder

Most abundant white blood cell, first to arrive at infection sites, engulf bacteria

Size 12-15 μm
Lifespan 5-90 hours
Location Blood, tissues
Phagocytosis NET formation Degranulation

Natural Killer Cell

Virus Fighter

Destroy virus-infected cells and tumor cells without prior sensitization

Size 10-16 μm
Lifespan 7-10 days
Location Blood, spleen
Cytotoxic killing Cytokine production Immune regulation

Dendritic Cell

Antigen Presenter

Capture, process, and present antigens to T cells, bridge innate and adaptive immunity

Size 15-20 μm
Lifespan Days to weeks
Location Tissues, lymph nodes
Antigen presentation T cell activation Immune tolerance
