What are Keystone Species?

Species with disproportionate influence on their ecosystems

The Keystone Concept

A keystone species is one whose impact on its ecosystem is much larger than its abundance or biomass would suggest. Like the keystone in an arch, these species hold the entire structure together.

"When you remove the keystone, the arch collapses. Similarly, removing a keystone species causes the ecosystem to change dramatically."

- Robert T. Paine, Ecologist

Disproportionate Impact

Small populations have large ecosystem effects

Ecosystem Regulation

Control populations of other species

Habitat Creation

Create or modify habitats for others

Types of Keystone Species

Different ways species exert their influence

Predators

Control prey populations and prevent any one species from dominating

Example: Gray Wolves in Yellowstone

Ecosystem Engineers

Physically modify habitats, creating opportunities for other species

Example: Beavers and their dams

Mutualists

Provide essential services like pollination or seed dispersal

Example: Bees and flowering plants

Iconic Examples

Famous keystone species and their impacts

Gray Wolf

Yellowstone National Park

Reintroduction in 1995 transformed the entire Yellowstone ecosystem. Wolves controlled elk populations, allowing willow and aspen to recover, which stabilized riverbanks and created habitat for birds and beavers.

Ecosystem Change Dramatic recovery
Trophic Cascade Full restoration

Sea Otter

Pacific Kelp Forests

By eating sea urchins, otters prevent overgrazing of kelp forests. Healthy kelp forests provide habitat for fish, sequester carbon, and protect coastlines from erosion.

Kelp Forest Size 20x larger
Biodiversity 50% higher

Beaver

North American Wetlands

Through dam building, beavers create wetlands that support diverse wildlife, improve water quality, and increase water storage. Their engineering benefits hundreds of species.

Species Supported 200+ species
Water Storage Increased 40%
