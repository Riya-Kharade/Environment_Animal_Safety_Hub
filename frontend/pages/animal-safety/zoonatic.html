Disease Transmission Pathways

How pathogens jump between species and reach human populations through environmental interfaces

Direct Transmission

Close contact with infected animals through bites, scratches, or handling. Includes saliva, blood, and bodily fluids.

Examples: Rabies (animal bites), Avian Influenza (poultry handling), Brucellosis (livestock contact)

Vector-Borne Transmission

Pathogens transmitted by arthropod vectors like mosquitoes, ticks, and fleas. Climate change expands vector ranges.

Historical Range Expanded Range
  • Temperature sensitive: Vector reproduction rates increase with warming
  • Range expansion: Moving into previously temperate zones

Environmental Transmission

Pathogens in soil, water, or air. Includes contaminated water supplies, aerosolized particles, and food sources.

Climate-Disease Nexus

How environmental changes create perfect conditions for disease emergence and spread

Temperature Rise

Warmer temperatures accelerate pathogen replication, expand vector ranges, and stress animal immune systems.

Prevention & One Health Approach

Integrated strategies that protect human, animal, and environmental health simultaneously

1

Ecosystem Protection

Preserve natural habitats to maintain biodiversity and reduce wildlife-human contact. Buffer zones between wild and populated areas.

  • Maintain wildlife corridors
  • Protect natural predators
  • Sustainable land use planning
2

Animal Health Surveillance

Monitor wildlife and livestock health for early detection of emerging diseases. One Health surveillance networks.

  • Wildlife disease monitoring
  • Livestock vaccination programs
  • Community reporting systems
3

Human Behavioral Changes

Reduce high-risk activities and improve hygiene practices. Education on safe interactions with wildlife and domestic animals.

  • Safe food handling practices
  • Vector bite prevention
  • Responsible pet ownership
