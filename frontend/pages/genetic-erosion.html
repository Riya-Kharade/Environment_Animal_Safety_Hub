The Silent Threat: Genetic Diversity Loss

Genetic erosion occurs when small, isolated populations lose their genetic variation over time. This reduction in diversity diminishes the population's ability to adapt to environmental changes, resist diseases, and maintain long-term viability. Understanding and preventing genetic erosion is crucial for effective conservation management.

Case Studies: Genetic Erosion in Action

Real-world examples demonstrate the consequences of genetic erosion and the success of intervention strategies.

Florida Panther

Genetic Rescue Success

Population reduced to 20-30 individuals, suffering severe inbreeding depression. Genetic rescue through Texas cougar introductions restored genetic diversity.

Heterozygosity: +45%
Survival: +20%
Kitten Survival: +50%

Isle Royale Wolves

Ongoing Erosion

Isolated island population of 2-50 wolves experiencing genetic erosion. Low genetic diversity increases disease susceptibility and reduces adaptability.

Heterozygosity: 0.22
Inbreeding: High
Extinction Risk: Elevated

Addo Elephant

Managed Metapopulation

South African reserve maintains genetic diversity through managed translocations between subpopulations, preventing erosion.

Heterozygosity: Stable
Gene Flow: Active
Population: Growing

Tasmanian Devil

Disease + Erosion

Low genetic diversity exacerbated devil facial tumor disease. Population crash from 150,000 to ~25,000 individuals in 20 years.

Heterozygosity: Very Low
Disease Impact: Severe
Recovery: Challenging
