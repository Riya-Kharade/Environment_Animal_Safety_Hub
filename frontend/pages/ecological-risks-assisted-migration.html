The Double-Edged Sword of Assisted Migration

Assisted migration offers a lifeline to species threatened by climate change, but relocating species to new areas can create unforeseen ecological disruptions. From outcompeting native species to altering entire food webs, the risks of unintended consequences must be carefully weighed against the benefits of species survival.

Understanding the Risks

üèÜ Competitive Exclusion

Introduced species can outcompete native species for resources, leading to local extinctions. This is especially problematic when the introduced species has advantages like larger size, faster reproduction, or better adaptation to disturbed habitats.

üçΩÔ∏è Trophic Cascade Effects

Adding or removing species from food webs can create ripple effects throughout ecosystems. Predators might become overabundant without natural controls, or prey species might explode in numbers, leading to habitat degradation.

üß¨ Genetic Disruption

Introduced populations can interbreed with native species, diluting locally adapted gene pools. This genetic swamping can reduce the fitness of native populations and erode evolutionary adaptations to local conditions.

üåø Invasive Species Transformation

Species that are beneficial in their native range can become invasive in new areas where natural enemies are absent. Without predators, parasites, or competitors, populations can explode beyond ecological carrying capacity.
