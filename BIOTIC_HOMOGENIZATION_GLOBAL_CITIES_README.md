# Biotic Homogenization Across Global Cities - Issue #2233

## Overview

This educational feature examines the global phenomenon of biotic homogenization, where urban ecosystems worldwide are becoming increasingly similar in their species composition. As cities expand globally, local biodiversity is being replaced by cosmopolitan species that thrive in human-modified environments, leading to the convergence of urban species assemblages across continents.

## Scientific Background

### Mechanisms of Biotic Homogenization
Urban environments facilitate the spread and establishment of similar species worldwide:
- **Global trade and transport**: Ships, planes, and cargo carry species between continents
- **Climate change**: Warming temperatures allow southern species to colonize northern cities
- **Urban habitat similarity**: Cities provide comparable niches across regions
- **Human-mediated dispersal**: Pets, ornamental plants, and escaped animals

### Convergent Species Assemblages
1. **Cosmopolitan birds**: Pigeons, sparrows, starlings in cities worldwide
2. **Invasive mammals**: Rats, feral cats, and urban-adapted raccoons
3. **Synanthropic insects**: Cockroaches, ants, and urban-adapted flies
4. **Ornamental plants**: Non-native species in urban green spaces

### Global Patterns
- **Northern Hemisphere**: European starlings and house sparrows dominate
- **Southern Hemisphere**: Similar patterns with local cosmopolitan species
- **Tropical cities**: Mix of native and introduced species
- **Island cities**: Particularly vulnerable to homogenization

### Consequences
- **Loss of local endemics**: Unique species replaced by generalists
- **Reduced ecosystem services**: Fewer specialized pollinators and seed dispersers
- **Decreased resilience**: Homogenized ecosystems more vulnerable to disturbances
- **Cultural impacts**: Loss of local biodiversity significance
